# Water Harvesting Techniques in the Sahel: Traditional and Modern Approaches

## Introduction

The Sahel — the semi-arid belt stretching across Africa from Senegal in the west to
Eritrea in the east — receives between 200 and 600 millimetres of rainfall annually,
almost entirely concentrated in a four-month wet season between June and September.
Managing this rainfall to sustain agriculture, recharge groundwater, and mitigate
flooding has been a central challenge for Sahelian communities for millennia.

## Historical Techniques

### Zaï Pits (Burkina Faso and Mali)

The zaï technique, practised by Mossi farmers in the Yatenga province of Burkina Faso
for at least 500 years, involves digging shallow planting pits — approximately 20
centimetres wide and 10 centimetres deep — in degraded, crusted soils before the onset
of rains in May or June. Organic matter placed in the pits attracts termites, which
break up the compacted soil and create channels for water infiltration.

A major revival of zaï in Burkina Faso beginning in the late 1970s and continuing
through the 1990s was driven largely by farmer Yacouba Sawadogo of Gourga village, who
began adding tree seeds to pits to reforest degraded land — a practice now called
Farmer-Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR). By 2004, FMNR had spread to over 5 million
hectares in Niger and Burkina Faso, with documented yield increases of 50 to 100 percent
in treated plots.

### Demi-Lunes (Niger and Mali)

Half-moon earthworks — demi-lunes — are semi-circular berms constructed on degraded
slopes to capture rainfall and channel it toward planted crops. Documented in Niger's
Tahoua and Maradi regions since at least the 1950s, the technique has been scaled
significantly since 2005 through partnerships between the government of Niger and
international NGOs including Oxfam and the World Food Programme.

Field assessments conducted in Tahoua region between 2010 and 2015 found that
demi-lune plots produced sorghum yields averaging 800 kilograms per hectare compared to
300 kilograms per hectare in untreated control plots — an improvement of approximately
165 percent.

### Swales and Contour Bunds (West Africa broadly)

Contour bunds — low earth embankments constructed along topographic contour lines — slow
and spread surface runoff, increasing infiltration and reducing erosion. Similar in
principle to the swale systems used in permaculture design globally, contour bunds have
been documented in northern Nigeria's Kano and Katsina states since colonial agricultural
surveys in the 1930s, where they are locally known as teras.

In the Kano Close-Settled Zone — one of West Africa's most densely farmed landscapes —
farmers maintained extensive networks of teras through the early 20th century. Colonial
disruption of communal land management institutions between 1900 and 1940 led to the
breakdown of these systems, contributing to accelerated soil degradation in the region.

A 2019 study by the International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics
(ICRISAT) assessed the hydrological impact of contour bund rehabilitation in Kano and
Jigawa states in Nigeria. Plots with rehabilitated bunds showed groundwater table rises
of 0.8 to 1.4 metres over a two-year period, and a 40 percent reduction in post-rain
flooding incidents reported by participating communities.

## Flood Mitigation and Agricultural Synergies

A critical but underappreciated function of these water-harvesting structures is flood
attenuation. In flat Sahelian landscapes, intense rainfall events — particularly the
episodic extreme events increasingly linked to climate change since 2000 — generate
rapid surface runoff that can inundate settlements and destroy crops within hours.

Swales and contour bunds intercept this runoff, storing it in the soil profile or
directing it to managed retention areas. Research from the Sahel and Sahara Observatory
in Niamey, Niger, published in 2021, modelled the effect of widespread contour bund
adoption across a 500 square kilometre catchment in Tahoua. Results indicated a 35
percent reduction in peak flood discharge during a 1-in-10-year rainfall event,
alongside a 25 percent increase in dry-season soil moisture across the catchment.

This dual function — reducing flood damage while improving agricultural water
availability — makes contour bunds and swales particularly valuable in contexts where
both food insecurity and flood risk are rising simultaneously, as is the case across
the Lake Chad basin and the broader Sahel since 2005.

## Integration with Modern Infrastructure

Several programmes since 2015 have attempted to formalise traditional water harvesting
into rural infrastructure planning. In Niger, the 3N Initiative (Nigeriens Nourishing
Nigeriens), launched in 2011, incorporated contour bund construction as a key component
of food security strategy, with 250,000 hectares treated by 2018.

In northern Nigeria, the Kano State Agricultural Development Project has since 2017
piloted community-managed swale systems in six local government areas, combining GPS
mapping of contour lines with community labour mobilisation for earthwork construction.
Preliminary data from 2020 showed that pilot areas experienced 60 percent fewer
post-rain flooding incidents than comparable untreated watersheds.
