# Lake Chad Basin: Climate Change, Shrinkage, and Regional Instability

## Overview

Lake Chad, located at the confluence of Chad, Nigeria, Niger, and Cameroon, was once one of
Africa's largest freshwater bodies. At its peak in the 1960s, the lake covered approximately
25,000 square kilometres. By 2001, it had shrunk to less than 1,500 square kilometres — a
reduction of over 90 percent in four decades. Satellite imagery from 2020 confirmed partial
recovery in the northern basin to roughly 2,500 square kilometres, attributed to increased
rainfall in the Sahel since the late 1990s, but the lake remains a fraction of its former extent.

## Historical Context

In the 10th century, Arab geographers described Lake Chad as a great inland sea, navigable
by large vessels. The Kanem-Bornu Empire, which controlled the Lake Chad basin from the 9th
century through the 19th century, drew much of its agricultural and commercial wealth from
the lake's fisheries and the fertile floodplains surrounding it. The city of Kukawa served as
the empire's capital in the 19th century and was a major trans-Saharan trade node.

By the 19th century, British and French explorers mapping the region noted the lake's
gradual recession. Colonial-era reports from 1903 to 1920 documented significant seasonal
variability, with the southern pool drying completely in some years.

## Drivers of Shrinkage

The primary driver of Lake Chad's shrinkage since the 1960s is reduced rainfall across the
Sahel region, linked to shifts in the West African Monsoon system. A severe drought between
1972 and 1975 caused the lake's surface area to fall from approximately 22,000 square
kilometres to under 7,000 square kilometres in just three years.

Secondary drivers include:

- **Irrigation extraction**: By 2000, upstream irrigation schemes in Nigeria and Niger were
  diverting an estimated 4.5 billion cubic metres of water annually from rivers feeding the lake,
  particularly the Chari and Logone rivers, which originate in the Central African Republic.
- **Population growth**: The Lake Chad basin population grew from approximately 13 million in
  1960 to over 50 million by 2020, dramatically increasing demand on water resources.
- **Groundwater depletion**: Shallow aquifer depletion across Borno State in Nigeria and the
  Diffa region of Niger has compounded surface water losses.

## Consequences for Agriculture and Livelihoods

An estimated 40 million people depend on Lake Chad directly for their livelihoods across
the four bordering countries. Traditional fishing communities such as the Buduma people,
who have inhabited the lake's islands since at least the 14th century, have seen their
catches fall by over 60 percent since 1983. Pastoral communities in Diffa, Niger and
Borno State, Nigeria have lost access to seasonal grazing lands that were inundated by
the lake as recently as 1975.

Agricultural systems in the region rely heavily on recession agriculture — cultivation on
exposed lake bed following seasonal flooding — a technique practised since at least the
15th century across the Sahel. As the lake's fluctuations have become less predictable
since 1990, this agricultural calendar has broken down in many communities.

## Link to Conflict and Displacement

Research published between 2015 and 2022 has increasingly drawn connections between
ecological stress around Lake Chad and the emergence and sustenance of the Boko Haram
insurgency in northeastern Nigeria, particularly in Borno and Yobe states.

A 2017 study by the United Nations Environment Programme found that communities displaced
from receding shorelines between 2000 and 2015 were disproportionately represented among
Boko Haram's recruitment base in Maiduguri, the capital of Borno State. Economic
marginalisation following livelihood collapse — rather than primarily religious ideology —
was identified as the dominant recruitment driver.

The Multinational Joint Task Force, established in 2015 and headquartered in N'Djamena,
Chad, has attempted to coordinate military responses across the four Lake Chad basin
countries, with limited success in addressing the underlying socioeconomic causes.

## Proposed Interventions

The Lake Chad Basin Commission, founded in 1964 and headquartered in N'Djamena, has
since the 1990s promoted the Transaqua project — a proposal to transfer water from the
Congo River basin via a canal through the Central African Republic into the Chari River.
The project would require approximately 100 billion US dollars and remains controversial
among hydrologists and regional governments.

Smaller-scale interventions advocated by the International Union for Conservation of Nature
since 2016 include community-managed water harvesting structures across the Sahel, improved
irrigation efficiency in Nigeria and Niger, and restoration of natural wetland buffers along
the Chari and Logone rivers.
