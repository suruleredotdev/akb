# Decentralised Financial Instruments in Sub-Saharan Africa: Informal Systems and Digital Bridges

## Rotating Savings and Credit Associations

Rotating savings and credit associations (ROSCAs) — known as esusu or ajo among the
Yoruba of southwestern Nigeria, adashi among the Hausa of northern Nigeria, susu in
Ghana and the Caribbean diaspora, and tontine across Francophone West Africa — represent
one of the most widespread informal financial institutions in sub-Saharan Africa.

Estimates from the World Bank's 2021 Global Findex survey suggest that approximately
34 percent of adults in sub-Saharan Africa participate in some form of ROSCA, compared
to 17 percent who hold formal bank accounts in rural areas. In Nigeria, the Central Bank
estimated in 2019 that the total annual value of informal ROSCA transactions exceeded
200 billion naira — approximately 500 million US dollars at that year's exchange rate.

## Historical Roots

The esusu system in Yorubaland can be traced in oral tradition to at least the 18th
century, though the concept of rotating pooled resources almost certainly predates
documented records. Missionary and colonial accounts from the 1840s onward — including
the journals of Samuel Ajayi Crowther, the first African Anglican bishop, published in
Lagos in 1854 — describe esusu as an established institution integral to trade financing
among Yoruba merchants.

In Hausaland in northern Nigeria, adashi systems were documented by British colonial
administrators in Kano and Zaria from 1903 onward as functioning alongside the formal
credit systems introduced by the colonial administration's Marketing Boards. A 1931
colonial report by Resident E.J. Arnett noted that adashi groups in Kano City managed
monthly pools exceeding £200 sterling — a substantial sum at the period — organised
entirely through oral agreement and social trust, with no written contracts.

## Structural Limitations and Failure Modes

ROSCAs function on trust and social sanction. Their limitations in contexts of high
mobility, urban anonymity, and economic volatility — all characteristics of rapidly
urbanising environments like Lagos, Kano, or Maiduguri — are well documented.
Default rates in esusu groups in Lagos peri-urban areas, studied by Esther Duflo's
research group at MIT between 2010 and 2015, ran at approximately 12 percent per
rotation, with near-total dissolution of groups following any single default event.

Additionally, ROSCAs provide credit at a fixed point in the rotation — the recipient
of the pool — but offer no mechanism for emergency liquidity outside that cycle, leaving
participants exposed during health crises, weather shocks, or market disruptions.

## Mobile Money and Digital Extensions

The emergence of mobile money platforms across sub-Saharan Africa since 2007 — beginning
with M-Pesa in Kenya — has created infrastructure that ROSCA organisers have adapted
for digital operation. In Nigeria, applications including PiggyVest (founded 2016) and
Cowrywise (founded 2017) have built digital ROSCA products that manage pooling, rotation,
and notification functions, reaching a combined 4 million users by 2022.

These platforms reduce default risk through automated deduction from linked bank accounts
or mobile wallets, extend geographic reach beyond face-to-face social networks, and
provide transparent transaction records — addressing three core limitations of informal
ROSCAs. They remain, however, dependent on smartphone ownership and bank account access,
restricting reach to urban middle-income populations.

## Blockchain-Based Experiments

Since 2018, several projects have attempted to extend ROSCA-like mechanisms through
blockchain protocols, particularly in Nigeria and Kenya. The premise is that smart
contracts on public blockchains — primarily Ethereum and, from 2020 onward, Celo,
which was designed explicitly for mobile-first financial access in developing economies
— can replicate trust enforcement without requiring social sanctions or intermediary
institutions.

The GoodDollar protocol, launched in 2020 on Ethereum, implemented a universal basic
income mechanism using yield from staked capital to distribute small daily payments
to over 500,000 registered users in 180 countries by 2022, with significant
participation from Nigeria and Kenya. While modest in absolute payment size, the
mechanism demonstrated functional on-chain distribution infrastructure reaching
users in economies with unreliable banking access.

Grassroots Economics, operating in Kenya since 2010, has since 2020 operated
community currencies on the Celo blockchain in Mombasa, Nairobi, and rural counties
including Kilifi and Kitui. Impact assessments published in 2022 found that
participating communities in Kilifi County showed a 15 percent increase in local
economic circulation — measured by transaction frequency within defined geographic
areas — compared to non-participating communities over a 24-month period.

## Remaining Challenges

Regulatory ambiguity around cryptocurrency and digital assets in Nigeria — including
the Central Bank of Nigeria's directive in February 2021 prohibiting banks from
servicing cryptocurrency exchanges — has significantly constrained blockchain-based
financial inclusion experiments in the country's largest economy. The directive was
partially reversed in 2023 following a regulatory review, but uncertainty persists.

The fundamental challenge for any digital financial inclusion mechanism in the Lake
Chad basin region — including rural Borno, Yobe, Diffa, and Lake states — remains
connectivity infrastructure. Mobile data penetration in these conflict-affected areas
remains below 20 percent as of 2023, according to GSMA Intelligence, limiting the
reach of both mobile money and blockchain solutions to precisely the communities most
in need of financial access alternatives.
