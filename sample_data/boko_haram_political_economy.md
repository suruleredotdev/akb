# Boko Haram: Climate, Economic Displacement and Insurgency in Northeast Nigeria

## Overview

Boko Haram — formally Jamā'at Ahl as-Sunnah lid-Da'wah wa'l-Jihād — emerged in
Maiduguri, the capital of Borno State in northeast Nigeria, in 2002 under the
leadership of Mohammed Yusuf. By 2014, the group controlled territory across Borno,
Yobe, and Adamawa states and had declared a caliphate centred on the town of Gwoza.
At its territorial peak between 2014 and 2015, Boko Haram held approximately 20,000
square kilometres of Nigerian territory and had displaced over 2.4 million people
across the Lake Chad basin region.

## The Economic Displacement Thesis

A dominant early narrative attributed Boko Haram's rise primarily to religious
radicalisation, framing the conflict as a product of Salafist ideology spreading
from the Arabian Peninsula through Nigerian Islamic networks. Subsequent research,
particularly after 2015, has substantially complicated this picture.

A landmark 2017 study by Marc-Antoine Pérouse de Montclos of the Institut de
Recherche pour le Développement found that the majority of Boko Haram recruits
interviewed in detention facilities in Maiduguri, Abuja, and N'Djamena between
2015 and 2016 cited economic grievances — unemployment, loss of fishing and farming
livelihoods, and displacement from Lake Chad's shrinking shoreline — as their
primary motivation for joining the group. Religious conviction was identified as
secondary or post-hoc justification in the majority of cases.

## Climate-Conflict Linkages

The connection between Lake Chad's shrinkage and conflict in the basin has been
documented in increasing detail since 2015. Key findings include:

**Livelihood collapse**: Between 1983 and 2012, fish catches in the Nigerian sector
of Lake Chad fell from an estimated 150,000 tonnes annually to approximately 50,000
tonnes. An estimated 8 million people in Borno, Yobe, and Diffa states lost fishing
livelihoods during this period. The Buduma and Kanuri fishing communities, whose
territories the lake's recession exposed to Boko Haram recruitment by 2010, had
no alternative economic base in the region.

**Agricultural disruption**: The Komadugu Yobe river — the primary water source for
agricultural communities in Yobe State — experienced severe low-flow conditions
between 1997 and 2015, linked to upstream irrigation extraction and reduced Sahel
rainfall. Groundwater-dependent farming in the Manga grasslands of Yobe State
collapsed progressively after 2000, displacing tens of thousands of pastoralists
toward Maiduguri and Damaturu.

**Displacement magnification**: A 2019 report by the UN Office for the Coordination
of Humanitarian Affairs traced the geographic progression of Boko Haram's
recruitment base. Communities within 50 kilometres of the Lake Chad shoreline as
it existed in 1975 — but now exposed by recession — were found to have
contributed disproportionately to the group's membership. The receded shoreline
had created a belt of economically marginalised, physically isolated communities
with no access to existing state services.

## Maiduguri as Convergence Point

Maiduguri, with a pre-conflict population of approximately 600,000 in 2009, absorbed
an estimated 350,000 internally displaced persons between 2014 and 2017. The city
had historically functioned as the commercial hub of the Lake Chad basin — a role
that had contracted significantly since the 1980s as the lake's shrinkage disrupted
trans-basin trade routes that had operated for centuries.

Mohammed Yusuf's original network in Maiduguri drew heavily from young men who had
migrated to the city from collapsing rural economies in Borno and Yobe states between
2000 and 2010. Labour market analysis conducted by Borno State Ministry of Economic
Planning in 2008 found youth unemployment in Maiduguri's Gwange and Bulumkutu
districts — Boko Haram's primary recruitment zones — at over 70 percent.

## Security Responses and their Limitations

The Nigerian military's Operation Lafiya Dole, launched in 2016 and coordinated with
the Multinational Joint Task Force from N'Djamena, achieved significant territorial
recapture by 2017 but failed to address the underlying conditions that sustained
insurgent recruitment. A 2021 assessment by the International Crisis Group found
that new Boko Haram recruits in 2020 and 2021 were drawn from the same ecologically
and economically marginalised communities as a decade earlier — demonstrating that
purely military responses remained insufficient.

Development economists at the University of Lagos and Oxford's Centre for the Study
of African Economies have since 2018 argued for integrated responses combining
ecological restoration in the Lake Chad basin, water harvesting infrastructure
investment in Borno and Yobe states, and economic diversification programmes in
Maiduguri — recognising that livelihood reconstruction is a prerequisite for
durable stabilisation.
